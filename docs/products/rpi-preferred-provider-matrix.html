The Non-Negotiables — What We Always Do / Never Do

Every rule below has a reason. Most came from a live case where violating it cost the client or the firm. These are not preferences — they are doctrine. Break one only with explicit JDM authorization.

🚫 RULE #1 — No MYGA Category

RPI does not sell MYGAs. The term is banned + the category is closed.
Why: FIA strategies (Nassau Growth Annuity fixed, NAC Control X fixed) beat MYGA economics across every meaningful horizon when paired with proper allocation. The MYGA label also makes the product sound commoditized — clients reduce it to "which bank has the highest CD rate" and we lose the relationship. Use the product name (Nassau Growth Annuity) or capability ("fixed-rate tax-deferred accumulation") instead.

🚫 RULE #2 — EBR Rider OFF Default on NAC Charter Plus

Enhanced Bonus Rider (EBR) never elected unless incoming money has real surrender charges the base bonus alone can't offset.
Why: Base CP10 = 12% bonus. EBR adds 9% more bonus BUT adds 0.95%/yr fee for all 10 surrender years — that's ≈10% compounded drag that wipes out the 9% incremental bonus. On liquid source money (CDs at maturity, matured annuities, cash savings), the EBR is net-negative. Only use when incoming money has ~5%+ surrender charges the base 12% can't cover.

🚫 RULE #3 — VC10 Allocation LOCKED at 50/50

NAC VersaChoice 10 High-band default = 50% Annual S&P 500 Cap + 50% S&P MARC 5% ER Participation. No MS DGI. No Barclays volatility control. No "enhanced participation with 0.95% fee" add-ons.
Why: RPI-standard allocation = consistent client pitch, consistent output across cases. S&P 500 Cap is household-name + client-explainable. S&P MARC 5% ER Participation = volatility-managed diversifier with upside participation. 50/50 balance behaves well across market regimes. MS DGI, Sunrise, Barclays, etc. are carrier capabilities, not RPI allocation defaults.

🚫 RULE #4 — GLWB "Joint Life" ≠ Joint Annuitant

On FIA products with a GLWB rider, "Joint Life" vs "Single Life" is a factor selection at rider activation — not a base-contract designation. Comparing apples-to-apples means same rider lens on both products.
Why: IRAs can't have joint annuitants (tax rules). Carriers build joint-life coverage into the rider mechanics separately — joint-life factor runs ~50bps below single-life factor. Don't frame a legacy contract as "no survivor protection" just because the spouse isn't a joint annuitant — check whether the rider has joint-life election first.

🚫 RULE #5 — Never Ask Carriers for Illustrations

RPI runs illustrations in-house using JDM's monthly factor feed. No carrier-rep illustration requests. No "can you send me an illustration with X allocation" emails.
Why: Carrier reps are salespeople. The second we ask for an illustration, we become a lead for their products, not an independent advisor. We have the factor data. We run our own numbers. We control the allocation narrative. Period.

🚫 RULE #6 — Anniversary-Before-1035

Before initiating a 1035 exchange, check whether the incoming contract is within 60 days of its policy anniversary. Do the exchange AFTER the anniversary credits hit if possible — never sacrifice a free anniversary credit.
Why: Index-crediting FIAs credit on anniversary only. Exchanging mid-anniversary-year = losing the credit that would have hit in days/weeks. Client gives up real money to save zero time. Flag every 1035 with the source-contract anniversary date before scheduling paperwork.

🚫 RULE #7 — No Fabricated Contact Names

Never generate rep, advisor, CPA, attorney, or carrier-contact names. If the name isn't in the client file, use a placeholder: [Role — TBD per JDM].
Why: Hallucinated names end up in client-facing documents, get spoken in meetings, and become embedded in case history. One hallucinated attorney name = one credibility destroyer. Placeholder and ask is always cheaper than guess and embed.

🚫 RULE #8 — Don't Write Nassau Bonus Annuity Plus

The "Plus" variant layers enhanced bonus/rider mechanics but pays for them by lowering caps, lowering participation rates, adding spreads, and stacking fees. Net: the extra bonus gets more than given back.
Why: If the case needs a bonus + income rider, the correct answer is NAC Charter Plus 10 (base, no EBR) + income need addressed via BenefitSolutions 10 or Nassau Income Accelerator — not a degraded chassis.

🧭 The Umbrella Principle

Every doctrine above flows from one posture: don't pay for features that don't add net value for the specific case shape. Bonuses that drag returns. Indexes that sound fancy but underperform simple ones. Riders that cost more than they save. When in doubt, strip the case to base economics and rebuild only what the client's actual need demands.

Preferred Carriers — Tier + Lineup

RPI's carrier stack is led by NAC and Nassau, with a tier-2 supporting cast for niche cases. Every carrier below has passed RPI's financial-strength and product-quality screen. "Tier 1" = lead with this. "Tier 2" = use when tier 1 doesn't fit the shape.

NAC (North American)

Tier 1 · Annuity Core
Use for: Accumulation + bonus (Charter Plus 10/14), accumulation no-bonus (Control X, VersaChoice 10, Performance Choice 8), GMIB income stacking (BenefitSolutions 10), dedicated income rider (Income Pay Pro 10), aged-out fallback (Guaranteed Allocation 5/7).
Strength: A+ AM Best · Strongest fixed rates in market (CX 3.90%) · 18+ uncapped indexes · 10% free withdrawal year 2+ · 100% confinement waiver.

Strength: A- AM Best · Income rider pricing competitive with NAC GMIB · Growth Annuity has shortest surrender in Tier-1 stack (7yr option).

Ameritas

Tier 2 · Age 81+ GMIB Only
Use for: Clients aged 81+ who need guaranteed lifetime income and no longer qualify for NAC/Nassau flagship products. This is the narrow case — everyone under 81 should be on NAC or Nassau.
Strength: A AM Best · Issue-age window extends to 85 · Rider pricing tolerable for the narrow cohort.
