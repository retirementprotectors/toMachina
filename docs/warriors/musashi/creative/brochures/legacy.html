Legacy · Estate + Trust + Beneficiary

Passing It On,
On Purpose.

Wills, trusts, beneficiary audits — the documents and decisions that protect everything you’ve built.

Your legacy is more than the accounts you own — it’s the life you want your family to continue living. A good legacy plan makes the hard decisions ahead of time, so your family isn’t making them in grief. This guide walks you through the four documents every adult needs, the difference between a will and a trust, and the beneficiary audit that matters more than either.

Conversation 1

The document conversation

Will, Living Trust, Durable Power of Attorney, Healthcare Directive. Every adult needs all four. Having one or two isn’t enough.

Conversation 2

The beneficiary conversation

Named beneficiaries on IRAs, 401(k)s, life insurance, and annuities override your will. Audit them every year — life changes fast.

Conversation 3

The tax conversation

Estate tax (federal + state), income tax on inherited retirement accounts, and step-up in basis for taxable accounts. The rules matter.

The Four Essential Documents

Document 1

Last Will and Testament

Directs assets NOT governed by beneficiary designation. Appoints guardians for minor children. Goes through probate — a public court process that takes time and costs money.

Document 2

Living Trust

Holds assets during life and transfers them at death without probate. Revocable vs. Irrevocable. Offers privacy, speed, and more control over how and when heirs receive assets.

Document 3

Durable Power of Attorney

Names someone to manage your finances if you become incapacitated. “Durable” means it survives incapacity — a standard POA may not. Critical before you need it.

Document 4

Healthcare Directive + HCPOA

Documents your end-of-life wishes and names a healthcare agent to speak for you. Critical for ICU scenarios where you cannot speak for yourself. Do not leave this to chance.

Trust Types Explained

Trust Type What It Does & When to Use It
Revocable Living Trust Grantor keeps control. Avoids probate. Does not protect from estate tax or creditors. Most common starting point.
Irrevocable Trust Assets removed from taxable estate. Estate tax + asset protection. Difficult to change once executed. Requires planning.
Special Needs Trust For beneficiaries receiving means-tested government benefits (Medicaid, SSI). Preserves eligibility. Essential if a dependent has a disability.
Charitable Remainder Trust Income to grantor for life; remainder passes to charity at death. Provides income + estate and income tax benefits.
ILIT (Irrev. Life Insurance Trust) Removes life insurance death benefit from your taxable estate. Classic high-net-worth tool for liquidity at death without estate tax.
Testamentary Trust Created by your will, funded at death. Goes through probate. Useful for controlling distributions to minor children or young adults.

Trust selection depends on your estate size, family structure, and state of residence. We coordinate with estate planning attorneys in our network or yours.

The Annual Review

Every year — around your birthday or tax time — review beneficiaries, update documents if life changed, and ask about new tools (SECURE Act updates, state law changes). One hour. One calendar reminder. Done.

Life Events That Require Updates

  • Marriage
  • Divorce
  • Birth or adoption
  • Death of spouse or beneficiary
  • Significant asset changes
  • Move to a new state
  • Business sale or formation
  • Adult child’s divorce
  • Health diagnosis
  • Inheritance
