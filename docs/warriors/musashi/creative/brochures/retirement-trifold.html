Retirement · Tax Strategy

The Roth
Conversion Window.

Why conversions matter: A Roth conversion moves money from a pre-tax account to a Roth, paying income tax now in exchange for tax-free growth and distributions forever after. Done right, it permanently removes assets from RMD exposure.

When to convert: The best window is the gap between retirement and age 73 — after your paycheck stops but before RMDs begin. Income often drops, pushing you into a lower bracket. Fill the bracket each year without jumping to the next rate. Social Security timing matters too: converting before benefits start means less ordinary income stacking.

How much: Convert enough each year to fill your current bracket without crossing into the next one. Run the number against your projected RMDs — converting now can reduce future mandatory withdrawals that would be taxed at higher rates. Watch the IRMAA thresholds: a conversion that pushes MAGI too high triggers Medicare premium surcharges two years later. The 5-year rule applies separately to each conversion for earnings access before 59½.

Retirement · Step by Step

Three Decisions

Every pre-retiree and retiree walks through the same three layers. Here's how each one works — and why the order matters.

Step 1

Know your accounts

Pre-tax (Traditional IRA, 401(k), 403(b), 457, TSP, SEP, SIMPLE) vs. Roth (after-tax contributions, tax-free growth) vs. Taxable brokerage (capital gains treatment). Each bucket has different withdrawal rules and tax treatment.

Step 2

Know your income

Systematic withdrawals, annuities (FIA, MYGA, variable), Social Security coordination, and RMDs. The sequence of withdrawals determines your lifetime tax bill — we model it before you retire.

Step 3

Know your taxes

1035 exchanges (tax-free between annuities/life), Roth conversion windows, RMD timing, 72(t) SEPP — the levers that determine what you actually keep. See back panel for the Roth conversion playbook.

4 Account Types

What Each Account Does

Traditional IRA

Pre-Tax

Tax-deferred growth. Ordinary income on distributions. RMDs at 73.

Roth IRA

After-Tax

Tax-free growth and distributions. No RMDs during owner's life. 5-year rule on earnings.

Employer Plans

401(k) / 403(b)

Pre-tax or Roth. Employer match. Can roll to IRA at separation.

Non-Qualified Annuity

After-Tax

Tax-deferred growth. Ordinary income on earnings only. 1035 exchange preserves basis.

72(t) SEPP — Before 59½

Withdraw without the 10% penalty using Amortization, Annuitization, or RMD-based payments. Minimum 5 years or until 59½. Any deviation = retroactive penalty.

Beneficiary Rules (Post-SECURE Act)

  • Spouse: Roll to own IRA, delay RMDs to age 73
  • Non-spouse: 10-year payout rule (SECURE Act)
  • Eligible Designated Beneficiary: Lifetime stretch (minor children, disabled, <10 yrs younger)
  • Estate or Trust: 5-year rule
  • No beneficiary: 5-year rule via probate
