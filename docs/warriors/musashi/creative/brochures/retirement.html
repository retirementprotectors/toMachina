Retirement · Income + Tax Planning

Retirement, Planned.

Your income blueprint — Traditional IRA, Roth, 401(k), annuities, and the tax moves that make them work together.

Retirement isn't one account type — it's a stack. Tax-deferred, tax-free, and taxable. Pre-tax contributions, Roth conversions, Required Minimum Distributions, 1035 exchanges. This guide walks you through the moving parts so you can see the whole picture — and the tax strategy that lets it breathe.

Step 1

Know your accounts

Pre-tax (Traditional IRA, 401(k), 403(b), 457, TSP, SEP, SIMPLE) vs. Roth (after-tax contributions, tax-free growth) vs. Taxable brokerage (capital gains treatment).

Step 2

Know your income

Systematic withdrawals, annuities (FIA, MYGA, variable), Social Security coordination, and RMDs — timing each source changes your tax picture entirely.

Step 3

Know your taxes

1035 exchanges (tax-free between annuities/life), Roth conversion windows, RMD timing, 72(t) SEPP — the levers that determine what you actually keep.

4 Account Types

Traditional IRA

Pre-Tax, Tax-Deferred Growth

Contributions are pre-tax (deductibility phased out by income if you have a workplace plan). Distributions are ordinary income. RMDs begin at age 73. Rollovers from employer plans accepted.

Roth IRA

After-Tax, Tax-Free Growth

After-tax contributions; earnings grow and distribute tax-free. No RMDs during the owner's lifetime. 5-year rule applies to earnings. Income limits apply to direct contributions — backdoor strategies available.

Employer Plans

401(k) / 403(b) / 457 / TSP

Pre-tax or Roth variants. Employer match is free money — take it all. Higher contribution limits than IRA. Can roll to IRA at separation or retirement. 457(b) has no early-withdrawal penalty.

Non-Qualified Annuity

After-Tax, Tax-Deferred Growth

After-tax contribution, tax-deferred growth inside the contract. Ordinary income only on the earnings portion (not basis). 1035 exchange moves the contract tax-free, preserving cost basis. No contribution limits.

Retirement · RMDs + Distribution Rules

When the IRS Comes Knocking.

Required Minimum Distributions, 72(t) exceptions, and beneficiary rules that protect your heirs.

RMD Uniform Lifetime Table — 2026

The IRS requires withdrawals from pre-tax retirement accounts beginning at age 73. Your RMD = prior year-end balance ÷ your Uniform Lifetime Table divisor. Missing or under-withdrawing triggers a 25% excise tax on the shortfall (reduced to 10% if corrected within 2 years).

Withdraw Before 59½ Without the 10% Penalty — 72(t) SEPP

Three IRS-approved calculation methods: Amortization, Annuitization, or RMD-based. You must take consistent payments for the longer of 5 years or until you reach 59½. Any deviation — stopping, changing amounts, or rolling the account — triggers the 10% penalty retroactive to day one. This is a one-way door. Plan carefully before starting.

Life Events That Trigger Distribution Decisions

  • Retirement (when to start drawing and from which accounts)
  • Job change / separation (roll to IRA or new plan?)
  • Disability (72(t) exception, no penalty)
  • Inheriting an account — spouse as beneficiary
  • Inheriting an account — non-spouse beneficiary
  • Divorce (QDRO required for employer plan division)
  • Death of account owner (triggers beneficiary RMD rules)
  • Reaching age 59½, 62, 65, or 73 (each has a rule change)

Beneficiary Rules (Post-SECURE Act)

Who inherits your IRA determines how long the money can keep growing tax-deferred. The wrong beneficiary designation can compress a 40-year stretch into 10 years — or even 5.
